About Ugo

Japanese blackwork. Irezumi iconography executed in shadow.

Darkness as Discipline

Ugo works at the intersection of Japanese irezumi tradition and blackwork intensity. Based at Guru Tattoo in San Diego — one of the most respected private studios on the West Coast — he has spent over a decade refining a style that takes the mythological iconography of Japanese tattooing and strips it to its heaviest, darkest expression.

Hannya masks. Peonies. Cranes in flight. Sakura caught in wind. Clouds that roll like smoke. The subjects are centuries old. But where traditional irezumi celebrates color — vivid reds, blues, golds — Ugo's work lives in the black. Heavy saturation. Monochromatic compositions. High contrast that carves form from shadow. It's irezumi through a gothic lens.

"I want the tattoo to feel like it was always there — like it surfaced from inside the skin, not placed on top of it."

His approach to composition follows the body's natural architecture. Full sleeves, back pieces, leg sleeves, and bodysuits that wrap and flow with muscle and bone rather than fighting it. Every element — from the curl of a peony petal to the snarl of a Hannya — is designed for its specific location on the body.

Ugo has tattooed at conventions internationally, including Mondial du Tatouage in Paris and across North America. But the core of his practice remains at Guru Tattoo, where large-scale Japanese blackwork projects unfold over multiple sessions — each one building toward a unified, immersive composition.

The work is ornamental in its precision and brutal in its weight. Not decorative. Not gentle. Permanent in every sense.
